Conflict of Interest Investigation

The Interim Pipeline

How a charter school consultant's undisclosed dual role at a service vendor raises conflict-of-interest questions at an 1,800-student Austin public school — and why the same placement pattern has appeared before.

By Mark Garcia
With reporting contributions from fellow NYOS Charter School parents
Published April 19, 2026

When Dr. Alan Seay stepped into the role of Interim Executive Director at NYOS Charter School in late February 2024, the Austin school's board and parents had reason to believe they were hiring an experienced, independent hand — a veteran educator who had led turnarounds at multiple Texas charter schools. What the community was not told, and what public records now make clear, is that Dr. Seay simultaneously held a formal advisory position at Dynamic Support Solutions, the Dallas-area firm that provides financial, accounting, payroll, and technology services to charter schools across Texas — and that subsequently received contracts from NYOS itself.

The dual affiliation is not buried in rumor or hearsay. It is published on DSS's own corporate website, where Dr. Seay is listed as "Special Advisor, School Leadership" alongside the firm's CEO and founder.1 His biography on that page specifically names NYOS Charter School as one of the institutions where he served as Interim Superintendent.2

This investigation, based on publicly available IRS filings, corporate web pages, board meeting records, and information contributed by NYOS parents, documents the verifiable connections between Dr. Seay, DSS, and NYOS Charter School. It distinguishes clearly between what is established by public records and what remains unconfirmed pending the outcome of active public information requests.

I. What the Tax Filing Reveals

NYOS Charter School Inc.'s most recent IRS Form 990, covering the fiscal year from July 1, 2023 through June 30, 2024, was filed on May 13, 2025 and is publicly available through ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer.3 The filing lists Dr. Seay as "Interim Exec. Director (thru 06/24)" working 40 hours per week — a full-time leadership position atop a $20 million organization serving approximately 1,800 students.

His reported compensation from NYOS across all three disclosure columns is zero dollars.

Confirmed — IRS Form 990, Part VII

Alan Seay is listed as Interim Exec. Director (thru 06/24), 40 hours/week. Reportable compensation from NYOS: $0. Compensation from related organizations: $0. Other compensation: $0.4

A person working full-time as the top executive of a $20 million public school reporting zero compensation to the IRS means he was not compensated directly by NYOS via W-2 wages or a 1099 issued in his name. His compensation was almost certainly routed through an intermediary entity — either his own consulting firm, Leadership4School LLC, or through DSS, or both.

The 990 also lists three independent contractors who received more than $100,000: DTK Facility Services ($429,835 for custodial), QA Construction Service ($250,759), and Speech Pathology Prn ($119,838).5 Neither Leadership4School LLC nor Dynamic Support Solutions appears on this list — meaning that if either entity was paid during this period, the amount was below the $100,000 reporting threshold.

Part IX of the 990 shows $486,591 in "Other" fees for services to non-employees (Line 11g) — a catch-all category that would typically capture payments to consulting firms, outsourced financial services, and interim placements.6 The individual vendors within this line item are not itemized in the filing.

II. The DSS Connection

Dynamic Support Solutions (DSS) is a Dallas-based firm that provides back-office services to charter schools, including accounting, CFO oversight, payroll processing, IT support, grants management, and audit preparation. The company states it has served over 200 organizations since 2011.7

Dr. Seay's affiliation with DSS is not speculative. It is publicly documented in multiple locations:

Confirmed — Public web sources

DSS Company & Team page (dssedu.com/company-team): Dr. Seay is listed with a photo, the title "Special Advisor, School Leadership," and a full biography that names NYOS Charter School among the institutions where he served as Interim Superintendent.1

charterschoolsconsulting.com: Dr. Seay has a dedicated profile page. This domain is a DSS property — its solutions page lists Gene Zhu as "CEO & Chief Accountant, Dynamic Support Solutions."8

Heritage Classical Academy (heritageed.org): Dr. Seay is currently listed as Interim Superintendent at this Houston charter school, with a biography mirroring his DSS profile.9

DSS's service offerings are broad. The firm advertises that its CFOs can "step in as an Interim or External CFO," and that it provides comprehensive support through bond issuance and capital financing.7 An interim superintendent who simultaneously serves as a Special Advisor to DSS occupies a position where he could influence a school's decision to engage DSS for precisely these services.

If the parent-reported dates are accurate, the pattern is striking: at both Legacy Traditional Schools and NYOS, Dr. Seay served as interim leader, and Dr. Rozas subsequently became the permanent superintendent. At both institutions, the transition introduced a leader who had not previously been part of the school's community. This sequence is consistent with a coordinated placement model — though this interpretation cannot be confirmed without reviewing the contracts and board deliberations that governed both engagements.

The parent-reported information about continuing engagements is equally notable. If Dr. Seay was conducting paid board training for NYOS in October 2025, and if DSS was sponsoring NYOS fundraising events in 2026, the relationship between NYOS, Dr. Seay, and DSS extends well beyond a short-term interim placement.

"The question is not whether interim consulting is appropriate — it often is. The question is whether the community was informed of the consultant's financial relationship with a vendor who stood to gain ongoing contracts from the school."

IV. The Conflict-of-Interest Framework

Texas charter school board members are prohibited from having a financial stake in a charter school management company, and board meetings must be open to the public.11 NYOS's own 990 filing confirms that the school maintains a written conflict-of-interest policy, that officers and key employees are required to disclose conflicts annually, and that the organization claims to monitor compliance.12

However, the specifics of that compliance monitoring are thin. Schedule O of the 990 describes the process as: "Board members and Officers of the organization are asked to voluntarily disclose any conflicts of interest as they arise."13 This is a passive, self-policing mechanism that depends entirely on the individual disclosing their own conflict.

The 990 answers "No" to Part IV, Line 28a, which asks whether the organization was party to a business transaction with a current or former officer, director, or key employee.14 If Dr. Seay's compensation was routed through DSS — an entity where he holds an advisory title — and DSS was also providing services to NYOS, the accuracy of this answer warrants examination.

Questions requiring public records to resolve

The following are not accusations. They are the specific questions that public information requests are designed to answer. Each can be resolved definitively with documents that NYOS is required to produce under the Texas Public Information Act.

How was Dr. Seay compensated?

The $0 figure on the 990 means NYOS did not pay Seay directly. The contract between NYOS and whatever entity provided his services would reveal whether DSS, Leadership4School LLC, or another intermediary was the compensating party — and whether that entity also held other service contracts with NYOS.

Was the DSS affiliation disclosed to the board?

NYOS's conflict-of-interest policy requires voluntary self-disclosure. Board meeting minutes from February–June 2024 would clarify whether any disclosure was made regarding Dr. Seay's role as Special Advisor to DSS.

Did Dr. Seay influence the decision to engage DSS?

Parent-contributed information indicates DSS contracts were voted on in December 2024 and July 2025 — after Seay's interim tenure. However, vendor relationships are typically initiated during the period when interim leadership is in place. Board minutes and any written recommendations from Seay's tenure would clarify this question.

What is the scope of DSS's current engagement with NYOS?

The total value of DSS contracts with NYOS, the specific services provided, and the terms of engagement are not publicly known. Vendor contracts and payment records would resolve this.

V. Dr. Seay's Organizational Track Record

Dr. Seay's resume describes more than three decades in Texas education. Several of the organizations he has led carry their own histories that provide context for evaluating his role as a school turnaround consultant.

A+ Charter Schools, Inc. (Dallas) — CEO, 2011–2016

Multiple versions of Dr. Seay's own biography state that he helped A+ Charter Schools "recover from years of TEA control."15 Being placed under TEA control is a serious state intervention reflecting significant governance, financial, or academic failures at an organization.

International Leadership of Texas — CEO, 2018–2019

ILTexas, now one of Texas's largest charter networks, has a 2.7 out of 5 Glassdoor rating from teachers — 28% below the education industry average.16 In 2023, the organization disclosed a criminal investigation involving a former teacher at its College Station campus.17 Dr. Seay's biographies credit him with executing what was then described as the largest charter school bond sale in history.

George I. Sanchez Charter School (Houston) — Interim Superintendent

George I. Sanchez holds an "Accredited — Warned" status from TEA.18 Only 13% of students scored proficient in math and 23% in reading.19

Heritage Classical Academy (Houston) — Current Interim Superintendent

Dr. Seay is currently listed as Interim Superintendent at this newly opened charter school while simultaneously listed on DSS's website as Special Advisor — the same dual-affiliation configuration that existed during his NYOS tenure.9

VI. Financial Context

NYOS Charter School operates with a total budget of approximately $20 million, funded almost entirely by public dollars — $19.4 million in government grants during the most recent fiscal year.20 The school ran an operating deficit of $489,240 in fiscal year 2023, following a prior-year deficit of $943,720. It carries approximately $29.1 million in combined mortgage and bond debt.21

In this financial environment, the selection of vendors for core business functions — accounting, financial reporting, payroll, IT, audit preparation — has material consequences for how public dollars are spent.

The NYOS school board is scheduled to meet on May 12, 2026. Parents and community members have the right to address the board during public comment.
